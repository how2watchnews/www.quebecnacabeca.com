A groundbreaking discovery has been made in a deserted quarry near Cairo, New York, where researchers from the University of Binghamton in the US and the University of Cardiff in Wales have unearthed the planet’s most ancient forest. The fossils, embedded in rocks dating back a staggering 385 million years, provide a unique window into Earth’s distant past. It is estimated that the forest once covered an area of approximately 400 km. If reports are to go by, this forest predates the Amazon rainforest and Japan\u2019s Yakushima Forest, which are some of the oldest forests in the world we know today.<\/p>\n

Through several studies conducted by palaeobotanists on the petrified roots found embedded in the region\u2019s rocks, it was declared that this is Earth’s oldest known forest. This incredible discovery unfolds the history of a time that existed long before human existence. Think of the time of dinosaurs when you think about this forest. Another thing about this forest that baffled researchers is the fact that, unlike their contemporary counterparts, the ancient trees of this forest didn\u2019t propagate through the release of seeds. They in fact, relied on spores for reproduction. This unique characteristic adds a fascinating layer to our understanding of prehistoric ecosystems. It\u2019s interesting to note that fungi share a similar method of reproduction.<\/p>\n

As researchers continue to delve into the secrets preserved in the rocks near Cairo, New York, this discovery promises to deepen our understanding of Earth’s early ecosystems, offering a glimpse into the fascinating world of ancient plants and the processes that shaped our planet over millions of years.<\/p>\n

India\u2019s Western Ghats ranges are also home to freshwater swamp forests that date back millions of years.
